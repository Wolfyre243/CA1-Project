01 What is Zero Hunger?

Picture this: A world free of hunger by 2030.

That's what Goal 2 (Zero Hunger) is all about. After all, the global issue of hunger and food insecurity is at an all time high, with close to 735 million people in a state of chronic hunger.

A hungry child enjoying his food

The second SDG, Zero Hunger, aims to safeguard safe and nutritious food for those in low-income regions, as well as to transform food systems, in order to achieve a sustainable world.

Investments will have to be made, especially in rural areas, to achieve this dream.

Climate Change

Droughts, floods, fires, heatwaves; the list goes on.

These are just a few examples of the extreme climates that drive people out of their homes. Not only that, climate change has had a great impact on food production across the globe.

This leads to decreased quantities and the quality of nutritious food being produced, hence affecting the food supply. This effect is further amplified in Africa, as they are unable to grow their own crops sustainably due to poor conditions.

Conflict


Both a cause and an effect of hunger, conflict plays a major role in the crisis of world hunger.
More than 85% of people experiencing hunger live in cities tainted with conflict, caused by disputes over food and water.
Moreover, conflicts disrupt harvests, decreasing crop yield and hence slowing down food production as a whole.

Many conflict zones in the world are in need of humanitarian aid, yet both parties who are taking part in the conflict may block off relief operations from reaching starving populations.



Click here to read more on conflicts and hunger!

Inequity

People with power define those who eat and those who starve.

The world is an unequal one; marginalised people within communities such as women, refugees, and those with disabilities are more likely to encounter numerous barriers to essential services such as jobs and services, but most importantly, supplies like food.

Thus, inequality has led to chronic hunger around the world, which in turn has deepened the problem of inequality.

World Hunger Programme

The United Nations World Food Programme (WFP) feeds the world's hungriest, most vulnerable people.

Some notable goals include aiming to feed 150 million people in 2024, delivering more than 15 billion meals per year.
Based on the problem of conflict in World Hunger, WFP provides food assistance to those regions caught in such conflicts as a result of weak or corrupt governments. Not only that, WFP also helps communities to relapse after conflict, helping them to strengthen governments and providing supplies.

WFP also takes in monetary donations from the public to aid in feeding those in need.

World Wide Fund

WWF (or World Wide Fund for short), has also been an active contributor in the fight against World Hunger.

WWF has dedicated itself to building innovations for sustainable development, supporting and promoting projects aimed at creating sustainable supply chain solutions for food companies.

Lastly, WWF partners with governments and other organisations to reduce key impacts of food production, convincing them to invest in sustainable food production.

Click here to donate!

The Hunger Project

The Hunger Project is a non-governmental organisation (NGO) dedicated to fighting World Hunger.

With over 1,200 project sites and 9,500 partner communities, The Hunger Project encompasses millions of people who strive to end hunger and poverty for good.

THP is focused on the Sustainable Development Goals, the most important one being Zero Hunger. In 2023, The Hunger Project trained over 59,000 people in food security workshops.
